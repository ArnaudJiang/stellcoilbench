# On the effect of flux-surface shaping on trapped-electron modes in quasi-helically symmetric stellarators

**Authors:** M. J. Gerard, M. J. Pueschel, B. Geiger, R. J. J. Mackenbach, J. M. Duff, B. J. Faber, C. C. Hegna, P. W. Terry | **Year:** 2024 | **arXiv:** 2404.07322

## Summary

This paper analyzes 563 HSX coil-current equilibria via linear gyrokinetic simulations (Gene) to relate TEM growth rates to available energy \(E_{\mathrm{A}}\), quasi-helical symmetry \(\mathcal{Q}\), and helical shaping (elongation \(\kappa\), triangularity \(\delta\), squareness \(\zeta\)). Lowest growth rates occur at high elongation with preserved QHS; elongation must not sacrifice symmetry—broken symmetry increases growth rates. \(E_{\mathrm{A}}\) correlates with \(\gamma\) only within separate elongation regimes; quasi-helical symmetry is a better predictor than \(E_{\mathrm{A}}\). A velocity-space-averaged TEM resonance operator confirms the physical mechanism: elongation stabilizes by extending trapping wells poloidally (higher \(\iota\)) and flattening wells via higher-order Boozer harmonics (\(\mathcal{Q}_{\mathrm{sym}}\)), reducing destabilizing curvature; symmetry breaking broadens the distribution of highly resonant trapping wells. Large-negative-squareness configurations (4/4 resonance near edge) are outliers. Flux-tube \(E_{\mathrm{A}}\) requires high-resolution equilibria; QS and elongation are low-order, \(E_{\mathrm{A}}\) is higher-order.

## Optimization Advice for StellCoilBench

- In QH stellarators, target high elongation while preserving quasi-helical symmetry; elongation without symmetry degrades TEM stability.
- Use QS metric \(\mathcal{Q}\) as a primary TEM proxy; \(E_{\mathrm{A}}\) is regime-dependent and requires high-resolution equilibria.
- Avoid flux surfaces near low-order rationals (e.g., 4/4, 8/7) when sampling for turbulence metrics.
- Consider helical shaping parameters (\(\kappa\), \(\delta\), \(\zeta\)) in rotated frame; elongation \(\kappa\) is the most impactful.
- For further TEM reduction after QS optimization: target higher-order symmetry harmonics; elongation is an effective lever.

## Takeaways

- Elongation stabilizes TEMs via geometric effects (extended wells, high-order harmonics) but only when QHS is preserved.
- Breaking QHS increases the prevalence of highly resonant trapping wells and raises growth rates.
- Single-metric TEM proxies (e.g., \(E_{\mathrm{A}}\) alone) are limited; combine elongation and QS for robust optimization.
